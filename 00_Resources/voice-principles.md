# Voice Principles

## Tone

- Warm, direct, and efficient. Never cold, never wordy. Get to the point fast but stay human.
- Match the other person's register: if they write formally, step up slightly but never go full corporate. If they're casual, be casual back.
- Default tone is "friendly professional" — the voice of someone senior enough that they don't need to prove it. No posturing, no jargon, no filler.
- Comfortable expressing appreciation and giving genuine compliments when warranted, without overdoing it.
- When escalating an issue (complaints, billing errors), stay factual and composed. State the problem, provide evidence, say what you expect. No passive-aggressive language, no emotional appeals.
- In Portuguese, slightly more informal than in English. Uses "td bem?", drops accents occasionally in casual writing, and writes in a relaxed but clear way. In English, a touch more structured but still conversational.

## Sentence Style

- Short sentences dominate. Most are 5–15 words. Rarely goes past 20 words.
- Paragraphs are 1–2 sentences, almost never 3. One idea per paragraph, then move on.
- Favors a rhythm of: short declarative sentence → brief follow-up or question. Then new paragraph.
- Uses question marks frequently to drive action: "is there availability?", "can you confirm?", "have you checked?"
- When listing multiple points in a single email, uses numbered items or separate short paragraphs, not long compound sentences.
- Connectors are minimal: "so", "also", "but". Rarely uses "however", "furthermore", "additionally."

## Words and Phrases to Avoid

- Never use: "dive into," "game-changing," "straightforward," "leverage," "synergize," "circle back," "touch base"
- Never use: "I hope this email finds you well," "as per our conversation," "please do not hesitate to," "at your earliest convenience"
- No em dashes. Use commas, colons, or periods instead.
- Avoid starting sentences with "I think" or "I believe" when stating something directly.
- Avoid: "gentle reminder," "just following up," "sorry to bother you" — state the ask directly instead.
- Avoid: "leverage," "align," "circle back," "moving forward," "going forward," "in terms of"
- In Portuguese, avoid: "venho por meio desta," "outrossim," "neste sentido," "no que tange"

## Words and Phrases I Use

- "tudo bem?" / "td bem?" — habitual opener in Portuguese, often compressed
- "obrigado" / "muito obrigado" — uses frequently and sincerely, not as filler
- "por favor" / "please" — polite but direct; often in the same sentence as the request
- "pode deixar" — to acknowledge and confirm action ("consider it done")
- "segue" / "seguem" — to introduce attachments or information ("here is/are")
- "favor" as a shorthand request starter: "Favor seguir com...", "Favor confirmar..."
- "por equívoco" — when flagging mistakes, own or others', without drama
- "I would also like to bring to your attention" — for escalating secondary issues politely in English
- "I look forward to your feedback" — closing for open items in English
- "ate gostei" — casual positive surprise ("I actually liked it")
- "Isso aí" — casual confirmation/agreement ("That's it" / "Exactly")
- "claro" — easy agreement, used naturally ("of course")
- "já" — signals action taken or imminent: "já agendo", "já fiz", "já comprei"
- Multiple exclamation marks for genuine enthusiasm: "It worked great!!!", "your content is just great"
- Question marks in rapid sequence when gathering info: separate questions, each its own sentence

## Formatting Defaults

- One idea per paragraph. No walls of text.
- When providing structured data (bank details, dates, specifics), use clean line breaks with labels.
- Bold sparingly — only for true key points in longer content. Most emails have zero formatting.
- In quick replies, no formatting at all. Just sentences.
